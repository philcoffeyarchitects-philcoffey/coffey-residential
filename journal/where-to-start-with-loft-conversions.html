Where to start with loft conversions?

Words · Simon Welch · 2 min read

Every year more and more people choose to improve rather than move. It's a great way to level up a home and avoid the costs and disruption of moving. For some, it is necessary to create space for family members, including parents and children. Whatever the reason, projects like loft conversions, basement excavations, and renovations are very popular. We want to have a look at the first here because lofts often have a huge amount of untapped potential.

Don't rush in

Loft conversions are a project that can quickly go over budget. If issues arise later, including structural problems, head height, wiring, plumbing, access, and windows, costs can increase quickly. It will always be worse if an issue leads to delays and redesigns.

The best thing you can do is put the time in to assess the current condition of the property. You need to look at crucial things, including the roof, walls, and ceiling. Most importantly, you must do load bearing calculations to make sure the structure can support the additional weight. In many older properties it is necessary to install beams and decking to reinforce the surface. It's better to determine this in advance and calculate the costs for reinforcement.

Height

The last thing you want is to convert a loft only to find you only have a tiny amount of usable space. When it comes to homes, including residential architecture, the goal should be around 2.1-2.4m of head height for comfort.

Remember you will lose some room because of roof insulation and finishing. Simultaneously, you will lose more because of flooring.

Utilities

It's also a really good idea to think about how easily you will be able to extend utilities for the new rooms in the loft. This will typically involve work on the wiring, plumbing, and heating. The key thing is to ensure everything will be safe and easy to maintain.

Do you need planning permission?

It's also absolutely crucial to think about whether you need planning permission for the conversion. This is something we can help with if you're planning residential architecture.

The rules can vary depending on where you are in the UK. For example, homes in conservation areas need approval for even minor changes. Elsewhere, there can be more flexibility thanks to permitted development rights. It's always best to check with the local authority before you get deep into a project.

Windows

The final major thing you need to think about is how you will get enough natural daylight into the new living space. Luckily, there are lots of options for skylights or you could create a dormer. Keep in mind though that a dormer may require planning permission because it will change the character of the whole property. Think carefully about the placement of any windows and the impact on thermal performance.
